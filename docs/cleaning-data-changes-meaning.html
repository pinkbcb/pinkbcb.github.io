When “Cleaning” Data Changes Meaning

Every data project involves cleaning messy data — removing outliers, filling gaps, and normalizing formats. But each of those steps is also a decision about what counts as “valid.” And those decisions can unintentionally alter the story the data tells. When we delete “inconsistent” records, are we correcting errors or erasing exceptions? When we fill in missing values, are we clarifying patterns or fabricating them?

Ethical data cleaning requires reflection and documentation. Analysts should always record their choices and justify them based on the goals of the analysis. Data integrity isn’t about perfection — it’s about honesty. The moment we start deciding what data “should” look like, we step into interpretation. Owning that responsibility makes our work both more transparent and more trustworthy.
